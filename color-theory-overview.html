Color Theory Overview

Color theory is the study of how colors interact with each other, their basics, their relationships, and their harmonies. Color evokes emotions, and can be utilized to influence how a person perceives a piece of work.

Below are 20 key color theory terms:

  1. Color Wheel: A visual representation of the relationships betweencolors arranged in an easy to understand format.
  2. Primary Colors: Pure colors that cannot be created by mixing other colors together. Colors include red, blue, and yellow.
  3. Secondary Colors: Colors formed by mixing two primary colors in equal parts. Colors include orange, green, and purple.
  4. Tertiary Colors: Colors created by mixing a primary color with a secondary color. Color examples include red-orange, yellow-green, and blue-purple.
  5. Triadic Colors: A group of three colors that are evenly spaced around the color wheel. Examples include red, yellow, and blue.
  6. Complementary Colors: Colors that are opposite each other on the color wheel. Examples include red and green, blue and orange, and yellow and purple.
  7. Split Colors: A color scheme that includes a base color and the two colors adjacent to its complementary color. For example, if the base color is blue, the split colors would be red-orange and yellow-orange.
  8. Analogous Colors: Colors that are next to each other on the color wheel. Examples include red, red-orange, and orange.
  9. Saturation: The intensity and purity of a color.
  10. Hue: The purest form of a color. There are 12 considered to be the most pure, the primary, secondary, and tertiary colors.
  11. Shade: A hue that has black added to it.
  12. Tint: A hue that has white added to it.
  13. Tone: A hue that has gray added to it.
  14. Value: The lightness or darkness of a color.
  15. Neutral Colors: Colors that are often perceived as the absense of color, but contain subtle undertones.
  16. Warm Colors: Colors that evoke strong emotions of warmth and excitement. Examples include red, orange, and yellow.
  17. Cool Colors: Colors that evoke strong emotions of calmness and soothing. Examples include blue, green, and purple.
  18. Monochromatic Colors: Variations of a single color, including different shades and tints.
  19. Color Scheme: A combination of all the colors used together in a project.
  20. Color Symbolism: The use of colors to represent ideas, emotions, or concepts. Colors are perceived differently across cultures.
